## Executive summary

This is Piccia's most foundational, most repeated topic, and that's fine, repurposing existing ground is standard practice here. But a plain restatement of "good design is accessible design" would blend into everything already published, including the AI/vibe-coding angle used twice already. The genuinely underserved angle, in Piccia's own back catalogue and across the whole watchlist, is accessibility at the design-system level: tokens, components and reusable patterns as the point of leverage, rather than fixing contrast and alt text page by page, forever.

## Recommended angle

Reframe "accessible digital design" from a page-level habit into a design-system-level discipline. Bake accessibility into tokens, components and patterns once, so every product built from them inherits it, instead of re-litigating contrast, alt text and focus states on every new page or feature. Use the WebAIM "same six errors, seven years running" data point as proof that page-by-page fixing doesn't work, then pivot to the systemic fix.

**Why this angle**: it keeps the proven, validated core message, but moves it to a level of abstraction nobody on the watchlist has covered and Piccia hasn't covered either. It speaks directly to Liz's real problem: not "does accessibility matter" but "how do I make it stick across a whole team without refighting the same battle every sprint." It gives Vicky real technical depth (tokens, components, contrast built in at the system level) delivered through design process, not a developer checklist, which is the exact differentiation Piccia's positioning claims.

## Suggested outline

1. Open with the WebAIM Million 2026 fact: the same six failures, unchanged for seven years, despite everyone "trying"
2. Name the real cause: teams fix accessibility at the page level, over and over, instead of the system level, once
3. What "baking it into the system" looks like: accessible colour tokens, component-level focus states and semantic structure, type scale and line-length set once at the token level
4. Why this is a design conversation, not just a developer one: owning token and component decisions is 80% of the accessibility work before a line of code exists
5. The organisational case for Liz: how this stops the endless re-explaining to every new hire and new feature team
6. Close on the creative brief framing: a well-built accessible design system isn't a constraint, it's a gift to every designer who touches the product next

## Unique advantage

Piccia's design-and-art-history-first background, not a compliance or developer background, makes her credible arguing that the system itself, not individual pages, is where accessibility should live. Real audit and training material (hundreds of audits, dozens of workshops) gives her specific, lived examples of the difference between page-by-page fixing and system-wide design, which a checklist- or tooling-led competitor can't replicate without inventing case studies.
