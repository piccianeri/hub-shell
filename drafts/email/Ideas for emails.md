## **Acronyms (done, on 1 April 2025\)**

Why acronyms are not accessible: never assume that everybody is going to know what an acronym stands for. For instance [these articles](https://observablehq.com/@frankelavsky/no-use-of-color-alone-in-data-visualization) that keep using CVD without explanation assuming that everyone knows that CVD means Colour Vision Deficiency. But if you do a search online, the first result for CVD is cardiovascular disease. So are you being accessible? No. You are not.

Perplexity has come up with this and I like it:

Let’s talk about acronyms. They’re everywhere—whether you’re reading an article, scrolling through LinkedIn, or sitting in a meeting. But here’s the thing: acronyms are *not* accessible.

Why? Because not everyone knows what they mean, and assuming they do creates barriers.

Take "CVD" as an example. I’ve seen it used in articles without any explanation, as if everyone automatically knows it stands for "Colour Vision Deficiency." But guess what? If you search "CVD" online, the first result is "Cardiovascular Disease." So now we’ve got confusion instead of clarity.

This isn’t just about CVD—it’s about acronyms in general. They can mean different things depending on the context, and not everyone has the same background knowledge to decode them. For people with cognitive disabilities, non-native speakers, or those using screen readers, acronyms can make communication harder, not easier.

So here’s my advice:

* **Spell it out first**: Always write the full term before using the acronym (e.g., Colour Vision Deficiency (CVD)).

* **Don’t overdo it**: If you don’t need an acronym, skip it altogether.

* **Think about your audience**: Are you sure *everyone* reading knows what that acronym means? If not, explain it.

Accessible communication is about clarity and inclusivity—not shortcuts that leave people behind. Let’s stop assuming and start making our writing accessible for *everyone*.

What do you think? Have you ever been confused by unexplained acronyms? Let me know—I’d love to hear your thoughts\!

“The design and marketing staff are keen to learn and implement accessibility, but management aren’t sold on it yet”.

My answer to the ignorant comment on accessibility being expensive: 

The other day I posted on LinkedIn

[Piccia Neri](https://www.linkedin.com/in/piccia-neri/) I have to disagree with you. The reality is that accessibility is a cost center and it's expensive. The few percentage points of customers you can possibly gain as a result is not worth it on its own, and in many cases is just purely hypothetical. If the web is to be accessible, it will need to become so by fiat and force, not expecting companies to do it on their own. 

[Michael Willman](https://www.linkedin.com/in/michael-willman-b789b1141/) Accessibility may well be expensive when a website is built disregarding it.

Websites that do so, however, usually have a host of other issues, because they bypass best practices in general.

For instance, if they ignore semantic HTML, at the basis of accessibility, or alt text for images, their SEO will also be quite bad (isn't that the case [Joost de Valk](https://www.linkedin.com/in/jdevalk/)?).

The best way forward is to build putting accessibility first, which results in leaner, faster websites with better SEO.

As for your "purely hypothetical" assertion re: customer gain: what data do you base this on? Plenty of research shows the opposite. I compiled some of it here: [https://docs.google.com/document/d/1b1\_VWW0Vui9hFyFx1nZnTsoxYzO1G\_SDaGZSsR2NhPA/edit?usp=sharing](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1b1_VWW0Vui9hFyFx1nZnTsoxYzO1G_SDaGZSsR2NhPA/edit?usp=sharing)

Ultimately those who persist building inaccessible websites will keep churning out subpar products: so much of accessibility is simply best coding and design practice. And they will also be behind the times.

Last but not least: the web was literally born to be accessible, see the origins of HTML. One of the creators of the web, Tim Berners-Lee said this in 1998: “The power of the web is in its universality. Access by everyone regardless of disability is an essential aspect.”
